🔄 What is the Charleston?

Right after the deal — but before the game starts — players do something unique to American Mahjong: they trade tiles with each other.

This is the Charleston. You pass tiles you don't want, and receive tiles other players don't want. The idea is that everyone has a chance to improve their starting hand before play begins.

It's the most distinctive ritual in American Mahjong. It's also the part that confuses brand-new players the most — so we'll go slow.

🧭 The big picture

The Charleston is a sequence of passes. Each pass: you choose 3 tiles from your hand, slide them face-down to another player, and they slide 3 to you.

The full Charleston is up to 7 passes:

1

First Right

Pass 3 tiles to the player on your right.

2

First Across

Pass 3 tiles to the player across from you.

3

First Left

Pass 3 tiles to the player on your left.

2nd Charleston starts
4

Second Left

Repeat — pass 3 to the left. (Optional — any one player can stop here.)

5

Second Across

Pass 3 across again.

6

Second Right

Pass 3 to the right again.

7

Courtesy

Optional — trade 0–3 tiles with the player across.

💡 Notice the symmetry: the second Charleston goes in reverse order (left → across → right). Easy to remember if you think of it as "out and back."

👉 Pass 1: First Right

The Charleston begins. Look at your 13 tiles and pick 3 you don't want — your "junk."

Slide them face-down to the player on your right. At the same time, the player on your left slides 3 face-down tiles to you.

✅ KEEP — 2-3-4 Bam
A potential consecutive run. These three tiles work together — never break a forming group.
✅ KEEP — pair of 5 Cracks
Pairs are valuable starting points — they can grow into pungs (3) or stay as the final pair.
❌ PASS — lone 1 Crack
Isolated, far from your other Cracks (5, 5). Hard to use without a lot of luck.
❌ PASS — lone 9 Dot
You have a 7-8 Dot pair forming, but a 9 sits at the edge. Sacrifice it for something better.
❌ PASS — lone West Wind
One honor tile by itself is dead weight. You'd need two more Wests to make it useful.
🤔 KEEP (for now) — E, N, D honors
Three different honors. Risky to pass them all on Pass 1 — they might pair up later. Re-evaluate after the next pass.
⚠️ Never pass a Joker. Jokers are gold — you'd be giving away your single most valuable tile.

↔️ Pass 2: First Across

Pass 3 more tiles — this time to the player across from you. They pass 3 to you in return.

You can pass any 3 tiles, including ones you just received from the right. The only hard rule: no jokers.

💡 By now you may be starting to see what hand-shape your tiles support. Use this pass to dump tiles that don't fit.

👈 Pass 3: First Left + the "blind pass"

Pass 3 to the player on your left. This completes the first Charleston (3 passes done).

But here's a wrinkle: by the third pass, you may not have 3 tiles you want to give up. That's where the blind pass comes in.

You can pass 1 or 2 of your own tiles plus 1 or 2 of the tiles you just received without looking at them — pushing them along sight-unseen.

The second Charleston →

🔁 The Second Charleston (optional)

After the first 3 passes, the table decides: do we continue?

If any one player says "stop," the Charleston ends and play begins. Otherwise, you do the second Charleston in reverse order:

  1. Second Left — pass 3 to your left
  2. Second Across — pass 3 across
  3. Second Right — pass 3 to your right
💡 If your hand is already shaping up nicely, calling "stop" after the first Charleston is a smart move — you don't want to give other players more chances to improve.

🤝 The Courtesy Pass (optional)

After both Charlestons, you and the player across from you can do one last optional trade. This is the courtesy — and it's the only "private" trade in the entire game.

How it works

  1. You turn to the player across and ask: "Courtesy?"
  2. You both agree on a number: 0, 1, 2, or 3 tiles. Both players must agree on the same number.
  3. You each select that many tiles, slide them face-down to each other, and pick up your new tiles.
  4. Done. Play begins.
💡 "Zero" is a perfectly fine answer. Most beginners say zero on their first few games and that's totally normal. The courtesy is a tool, not a requirement.

When to actually use it

The courtesy is powerful when you're one tile away from a specific shape. Example:

You're going for "even-numbered Bams" and your hand is almost there. You just need one more 8 Bam. The player across has been discarding Cracks all Charleston — they probably don't want yours either. Ask for a 1-tile courtesy: trade your useless 4 Crack for whatever they don't want. You might get lucky.

⚠️ Rules to remember:
  • Both players must agree on the count. If they want 2 and you want 0, you do 0.
  • No jokers — same as every other Charleston pass.
  • You can't see what they're sending you until you've sent yours. It's a blind trade.
  • The courtesy is only with the player across — never with the player to your left or right.

When to skip it

Say "zero" if:

  • You're not sure what hand you're building yet
  • You don't have any obvious "junk" left to trade
  • The player across has been making lots of exposures (they may know your tiles too well)
  • You're new and overwhelmed — there's no shame in "zero"
