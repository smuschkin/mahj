Jokers & Calling

Grabbing tiles, exposing groups, and using your wild cards

📣 What does "calling" mean?

During the game, players take turns drawing tiles and discarding ones they don't want. Normally, a discarded tile is gone — it sits in the middle of the table.

But sometimes, a discarded tile is exactly what you need. When that happens, you can call for it — say it out loud and grab it before the next player draws.

Calling is how you complete groups faster than just drawing from the wall. It's also one of the most exciting parts of the game.

🔔 The 3 things you can call

Unlike Asian mahjong, American Mahjong only has three things you can call. No sequences (no 1-2-3 grabs).

3

PUNG

You already have 2 of a tile. Someone discards a 3rd. Call "Pung!"

4

KONG

You already have 3 of a tile. Someone discards a 4th. Call "Kong!"

14

MAHJONG

The discard completes your entire hand. Call "Mahjong!" — you win the round.

⚠️ Anyone can call a Pung or Kong, no matter whose turn it is — calling skips the turn order. But you must call before the next player picks up a tile from the wall, or it's too late.
How calling works →
Screen 3 of 8

🗣️ How a call works

A call has 4 quick steps:

  1. Say it out loud. Just the word: "Pung!" or "Kong!" or "Mahjong!" Don't be shy — at the table, the call has to be heard before it counts.
  2. Take the discarded tile. Reach in and grab it. The other players will wait.
  3. Expose your group. Lay your matching tiles face-up at the front of your rack so everyone can see them. (More on this next.)
  4. Discard one tile from your hand to keep your tile count correct. Then play continues with the player to your right.
💡 You don't need to be loud or dramatic — just clearly say the word so others hear it before someone else moves on.

👀 Exposing: showing your tiles

When you call a Pung or Kong, you have to expose the group — lay all the matching tiles face-up at the front of your rack.

This means your opponents can see exactly what you took. It's a trade-off: you got the tile you needed, but now everyone knows part of your hand.

⚠️ Once a group is exposed, you can't take it back. Make sure you actually want the tile before you call.

⭐ Jokers: where they CAN go

You met jokers in Module 1 — the gold wild-card tiles. Now let's get specific about what they can and can't do.

✅ Jokers CAN substitute in:

  • Pungs (3 of a kind)
  • Kongs (4 of a kind)
  • Quints (5 of a kind — yes, that's a thing on some hands)
  • Any group of 3 or more identical tiles

❌ Jokers CANNOT substitute in:

  • Pairs (2 of a kind)
  • Singles (1 specific tile)
  • Any "Singles & Pairs" hand (no jokers anywhere)
7
7
JOKER

Example: 2 real 7 Bams + 1 Joker = a valid Pung of three 7 Bams

⚠️ The pair rule trips everyone up. If your hand needs a pair of 5 Cracks, you need two real 5 Cracks. A joker won't work.

🔄 The Joker Exchange (the magic move)

Here's a rule unique to American Mahjong — and one of its coolest features.

If another player has an exposed group containing a joker, and you have the real tile that joker is standing in for, you can swap your real tile for their joker. On your turn, before you discard.

You hand them your real 3 Bam. They give you their Joker. Now you have a Joker in your hand to use however you want, and their Pung is now made of 3 real tiles.

💡 Why this is awesome: Jokers are the most powerful tile in the game. Stealing one from another player is one of the best moves you can make.

Rules for the exchange

  • Only on your turn, after you draw and before you discard
  • You can exchange with any player's exposed group, including your own
  • You must give a real tile — not another joker
  • You can do multiple exchanges on the same turn if you have the right tiles
  • You cannot exchange with tiles that are still in someone's hand — only exposed groups

🤔 When SHOULD you call? (the judgment call)

Just because you can call doesn't mean you should. Calling has real costs.

✅ Good reasons to call

  • The tile completes a group you actually need for your target hand
  • It's late in the game and you're racing to finish
  • You're "calling for mahjong" — the discard wins you the game (always call!)
  • You can use the call to grab a joker via the exchange rule

❌ Bad reasons to call

  • You "might" need it eventually — wait until you're committed
  • It commits you to a hand you're not sure about (exposing locks you in)
  • You're early in the game and still flexible — keep your options open
  • It would expose information that lets opponents read your hand
⚠️ The hidden cost of calling: Once you expose tiles, you can't change which hand you're building toward. Some hands require concealed tiles (no calls). Calling early can lock you out of better options later.
One more thing →
Screen 8 of 8

🚨 Three rules beginners forget

These are the calling/joker mistakes that trip up almost everyone in their first 10 games. Burn them in:

1. You can't call a tile you don't actually need

If you call a Pung but realize you can't actually complete a valid hand, your hand becomes "dead" — you keep playing but can't win, and you owe each player a penalty at the end. Always check before you call.

2. Jokers CANNOT be called from the discard pile

If someone (foolishly) discards a joker, no one can grab it. The joker is dead. This is why nobody discards jokers.

3. You have to expose your call IMMEDIATELY

Don't grab the tile and slide it into your rack — that's not allowed. The whole group goes face-up at the front of your rack right away.
