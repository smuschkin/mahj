# FB Reply Playbook — American Mahjong Beginner Questions

A response bank for commenting in mahjong FB groups (and Reddit, IG, TikTok) without tripping promo rules. Goal: become the recognized "helpful beginner person" in the niche. Downloads follow. Promotion doesn't.

---

## The 5 rules (read this first, every time)

1. **Answer the question first.** Your reply must be useful even if you never mentioned MAHJ.
2. **Never mention the app in your first reply in a thread.** If someone follow-ups asking what you used, *that's* when you mention it.
3. **One app mention per thread, maximum.** More than that = spam pattern.
4. **"Free printable cheat sheet" > "download my app."** Softer entry, admins allow it more readily.
5. **No App Store links in comments.** Say "search MAHJ in the App Store" or "welcome2mahj.com." Links get filtered; plain text doesn't.

### 1. "I want to learn — where do I start?"

**Short (no app mention):**
> Honestly the easiest on-ramp is learning the 152 tiles first — sounds like a lot but it's really just 3 suits (Bams, Craks, Dots) x 9 values x 4 copies, plus winds, dragons, flowers, and jokers. Once you can recognize tiles by sight, everything else makes way more sense. The NMJL card is the harder part, but it clicks fast once you have the tile names down.

**Medium (no app mention):**
> Three steps that actually work: (1) learn the tiles by sight — 20 min and you've got it, (2) learn the Charleston (tile-trading ritual before play), (3) learn to read one hand on the NMJL card. If you can do those three things, you can sit at a table and someone experienced can walk you through the rest. The biggest mistake is trying to memorize the whole card before you've ever held tiles — do it in order.

**Longer (mention app only if someone else hasn't already in the thread):**
> The tiles take about 20 minutes to learn by sight. Then the Charleston (pre-game tile trade), then reading the NMJL card. Do it in that order. A few free resources: watch a couple of YouTube tutorials for setup, print a cheat sheet with the calling rules so you can glance at it mid-game. I made a free beginner app called MAHJ (search it in the App Store) because I was teaching friends and realized there was no structured beginner path — but honestly, whatever you use, the order matters more than the tool.

### 3. "How do I teach my friends?" / Teaching resources

This is your **highest-conversion question type.** People asking this are exactly your target — they want a tool so they don't have to teach from scratch.

**Variation A:**
> The trick is to not teach them at the table — it ruins the game for everyone and new players feel stupid. Give them something to learn *before* they show up. A printable cheat sheet for the calling rules, Charleston order, and joker rules gets them 80% of the way there. Then their first game is just "try it with help" instead of "learn while playing."

**Variation B (soft app mention):**
> Same struggle — teaching mid-game is brutal. What worked for me: have them learn the tiles and the Charleston ahead of time, then their first sit-down is just practice, not a lesson. That's actually why I built MAHJ (free app, search it in the App Store) — so I could tell friends "do the first 4 lessons and come over Saturday." Cut my teaching time at the table to basically zero.

**Variation C:**
> The game is too much to learn cold at a table. Anything that lets them learn tiles, Charleston, and reading the card at their own pace *before* game night is worth its weight in gold. I print a one-page cheat sheet and text it out ahead of time — happy to share it if anyone wants, DM me.

### 4. "Can you recommend a book?"

Don't push the app here — this person wants a book. Be helpful, build goodwill.

**Variation A:**
> For a first read I'd do Mah Jongg Made Easy or Gladys Grad's basic guide. They're short and they actually focus on how you think at the table, not just rules. After you've played a few times, Tom Sloper's site and columns are gold — he's the dean of American mahjong writing.

**Variation B:**
> Books are tricky because the NMJL card changes every year so a lot of them go out of date. Mah Jongg Made Easy is a good evergreen one. For strategy once you're past beginner, Tom Sloper's articles (free online) are better than any book.

### 5. "I'm confused by the NMJL card"

**Variation A:**
> Nobody isn't confused by the card at first — it looks like hieroglyphics. The trick is to not try to read the whole card. Pick one section (Like Numbers is usually the friendliest for beginners), learn to read *one hand* in it, and build from there. Once you understand how one hand is notated, the rest follow the same logic.

**Variation B:**
> Totally normal. It takes most people 2–3 games before the card stops looking like a wall of code. Things that help: (1) learn one section at a time, not the whole card, (2) understand that each row is ONE hand and you only need to make ONE hand total, (3) the colors on the card mean "these tiles must be the same suit as each other" — that unlocks a lot.

### 6. Joker questions

**"Can you use a joker in a pair?"**
> No — that's the #1 thing beginners get tripped up on. Jokers only work in groups of 3 or more (pungs, kongs, quints). Pairs have to be two real matching tiles. The logic is that jokers are "flexible extras" and a pair only has 2 slots, so there's no flexibility for them to fill.

**"Can you exchange a joker?"**
> Yes, once a joker is *exposed on someone else's rack* in a pung/kong/quint, any player can swap a real matching tile for it on their turn. You can't swap for jokers still in someone's concealed hand — only exposed ones. And once you take it, it's yours to use freely.

### 7. Charleston questions

**Variation A:**
> Charleston order: Right, Across, Left, then Left, Across, Right (that's the "second Charleston" — which is optional, anyone can stop it). Then the Courtesy Pass across the table is 0–3 tiles, by mutual agreement with the person across from you. Don't overthink it the first time — just pass your three ugliest tiles each round.

**Variation B:**
> The part nobody tells you: you're not supposed to just pass garbage. You're passing tiles that don't fit *your* hand so someone else can maybe use them. Which means you have to have some idea what hand you're going for before you start, even a loose one. Pick a section of the card you like, look at what tiles it wants, and pass everything else.

### 8. "Looking for a beginner group in [city]"

You can't help with local unless it's your city — but this is a perfect chance to mention starting their own group.

**Variation A:**
> Honestly the secret is you don't need to find one — you make one. Text 3 friends, pick a night, learn together. The best mahjong groups all started with one person deciding to host. Nobody has to already know how to play.

**Variation B:**
> Local groups are hit or miss depending on where you live. Sometimes the faster path is: find 3 friends who are curious, pick a Tuesday, and learn together. Most people who say "I want to join a group" are secretly willing to start one if someone else organizes it — so be that person.

### 9. "I'm intimidated / scared to play"

**Variation A:**
> Every single person at every mahjong table was terrible their first few games. The women who've been playing 30 years still mess up calls sometimes. It's way more forgiving than it looks — people at tables want new players, they're genuinely happy to help. The only thing that keeps people out is not starting.

**Variation B:**
> The intimidation is the biggest barrier and it's also fake — there's nothing about this game that a reasonable person can't learn in a week. The "mahjong ladies who've been playing forever" are almost all sweet and happy to coach. Start with people who love you (friends, family) and you'll build confidence faster than trying to join an advanced group first.
